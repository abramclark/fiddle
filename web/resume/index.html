About Me

My answer to "what're you going to be when you grow up" started out as "inventor", then later at age 6 became "scientist". In middle school I explored books and many articles on philosophy, microbiology, and subatomic physics. In my early teens, realizing real scientific research is extremely slow and tedious, I developed a strong interest in consciousness and artificial intelligence, complimented by an enthusiastic pursuit of programming which I started in 5th grade.

In my early 20s it became clear to me that strong AI, if achieved at all, would likely result in a totally opaque process that, at best, would be a poor and morally questionable mimic of a human mind. I decided to create a far more useful technology; intuitive software and development tools. As software subsumes more and more of our culture and society, people increasingly rely on software engineers and designers. Along with all engineers, I'm faced with the difficulties of working with the increasingly complex tools we are becoming increasingly dependent on.

More importantly, humanity's survival is becoming dependent on the majority of us making decisions based on a complex systems level understanding of our natural environment and economies.

In short, I'm most excited about tools for understanding complex systems and education, and building these is the most important thing I could possibly do.

Education

The foundation of my math, science, and technology came from my Dad. We had many "science projects" which eventually led to building countless circuits. Some noteable examples I remember were a tesla coil, an amplified oscilator switched by a photo diode with a tuneable threshold (which I designed), and finally for a 5th grade science project: a 4 bit binary adder composed only from ~50 bare CMOS logic gates, along with an explanation of this being a small but critical component of an arethmitic logic unit in a CPU.

As a self-taught programmer, I learned through immersion on a home PC starting at age 9 with QBASIC. Currently I'm a bit baffled about how this happened, as my family had no Internet and no conception of programming. I think my brother and I simply learned from the manual and various DOS programs that came with our 486 PC. Eventually realizing it wasn't possible to create smooth game graphics with QBASIC, I moved on to C, learning from the Borland C manual. After being introduced to the web at age 12, I immediately moved to Perl and then JavaScript.

In high school I took every available tech class, but there was no programming curriculum. I created 2 years of material for two independent studies, one with my physics and chemistry teacher, the other with my computer electronics teacher.

From there I started my working education at University of Washington, learnig from faculty and the IT department. For every career move I've found a role that stretches my skills and knowledge. I also actively research developments in the industry and in programming research.
